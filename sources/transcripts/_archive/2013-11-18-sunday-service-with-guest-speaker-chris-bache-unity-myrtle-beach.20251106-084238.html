Chris Bache: Good morning, everyone! I’m pleased to be here with you today. The last time I was here was on Labor Day weekend. Today, I'd like to talk about the topic of death—not just the fear that often surrounds it, but the reality and understanding of life beyond death.

Now, let’s begin with a meditation. Close your eyes and feel the ground beneath you—solid and supportive. Picture us all in a large circle, with each person placing their hand on the shoulder of the next as we share in this energy together.

Now, bring to mind someone you’ve lost and imagine them standing behind you, placing their hand on your shoulder. As we expand this circle throughout history, we connect to a larger community of life, both past and future. This cycle of birth and life naturally includes death, and it is part of our experience together.

As we gather today, let’s embrace the simplicity of life’s cycle and the shared experiences that connect us.

Now, I want to share some insights into the transformative research happening around death and to dispel some of the fears that come with it.

Chris Bache: The first two books I want to discuss were written by David Ring, a professor at the University of Connecticut. He was a founder of the near-death experience movement and established the Journal of Thanatology. His first book documented the phenomena of near-death experiences, while his second, Heading Toward Omega, delves into what we are meant to learn from these experiences.

David focused on individuals who had profound near-death experiences—those who didn’t just leave their bodies, go through a tunnel, or see a light, but who actually entered the light. He calls these accounts "core envy ears" and collects personal stories that are incredibly moving. In Chapter Three of Heading Toward Omega, he presents the experiences of thirteen individuals with very deep near-death experiences. Their narratives touch the heart and soul as these people describe having encountered the divine. Their lives change dramatically afterward; they undergo transformations in their mind, emotions, values, religions, priorities, and even their physiology.

His latest book, Lessons from the Light, sums up his extensive research career, examining cases of blind individuals who, during their near-death experiences, were able to see. He includes a chapter called "Returning to the Source," which highlights some of the deepest near-death experiences—extraordinary spiritual journeys.

Now, some may wonder if these experiences are simply an endorphin rush or a fantasy. To address this skepticism, I recommend the work of Pim van Lommel, a Norwegian psychiatrist. His comprehensive analysis of near-death experiences stands out, as he argues that every attempt to explain these experiences away fails to appreciate their reality. His findings endure scrutiny and provide solid evidence, which I often share with my academic colleagues who dismiss these phenomena.

Another remarkable account comes from Dr. Eben Alexander, a neurosurgeon who detailed his personal near-death experience in his book Proof of Heaven. His bravery in publishing this work is noteworthy, considering how he openly challenges the materialist views in his field.

I must also mention the concept of life-between-lives therapy, which Michael Newton pioneered. His books, Journey of Souls and Destiny of Souls, are essential reads for anyone interested in what happens after death. Newton discovered that he could guide his patients not only through their past lives but also through the time in between those incarnations. He notes that people go home to a spiritual community where they continue to evolve together, even if their individual journeys unfold at different paces.

Newton’s work helps people understand the choices they make prior to incarnating and how these choices connect to the soul's greater purpose. The soul’s agenda is far more comprehensive than our personal desires, often leading us to choose lives filled with challenges that foster growth.

Lastly, I want to touch on modern psychedelic research and its connection to spiritual transformation. Many indigenous cultures refer to these substances as "sacred medicines" rather than hallucinogens, emphasizing their role in helping people remember their true nature and the game of life. Stanislav Grof, a key figure in the transpersonal psychology movement, has contributed significantly to psychedelic therapy, particularly for the terminally ill. His works offer insights into death from both a historical and cross-cultural perspective.
