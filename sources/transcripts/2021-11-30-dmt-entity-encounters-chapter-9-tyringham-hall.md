Chris Bache
It’s a pleasure to be here, to experience all of your thoughts and energy and to be able to contribute to that pool. Anton, thank you for the invitation and for bringing us all together. David, Rory, thank you for making it happen and doing all the legwork that pulls us together. The day I got the invitation to come to this conference, when Christina came home, I said, “Hey, something interesting came in the mail today. I just got a wonderful invitation to participate in a conference on DMT and entity encounters in England.” She said, “Yeah?” I said, “Yeah—there are only two problems. I haven’t done much DMT and I don’t have entity encounters.”

So this started a long process of first reading everything I could read on DMT. I’ve done the DMT Summit. I’ve done ayahuasca, and I have had entity encounters—particularly with Salvia divinorum and in an ayahuasca session. I’m completely confident and comfortable with the idea of there being multiple levels of intelligence in the universe and multiple entity forms or encapsulations of that intelligence. I’m comfortable with that. But in my primary work—the LSD work I did from ’79 to ’99—the nature of that work always drove toward a fundamental singularity of consciousness. It’s not that this disqualifies or trivializes entities, but it’s a different texture. And so it’s led me to think about…

All of you know the distinction between psycholytic therapy and psychedelic therapy—psycholytic low dose, psychedelic high dose. After three preliminary sessions at 250 micrograms, all my work was at high dose—500 to 600, really about 600 micrograms. At first I thought I was just doing psychedelic therapy, an extended course. Of course, in the Spring Grove program they limited those sessions to one to three sessions. Working with doses that high is very demanding. It’s very unorthodox, and it may not even be recommended. Ralph Metzner—in his wonderful book, Allies for Awakening—recommends against taking doses that high. He says it’s overwhelming, tends to produce dissociated states, and tends to produce dysphoria. You get so overwhelmed by the intensity of the experience you can’t manage it. I think that’s good advice—wise advice—particularly if you’re working at a clinical level and you’re working to heal. There’s no need to shake up the system that hard and that severely.

I’m not a clinician, and I wasn’t really trying to heal. I’m a philosopher. I was trained as a philosopher. I wanted to explore the universe. I adopted the high-dose method partly because I was in a dual-career marriage and it was hard to get time for a session day, so I wanted to make the most out of every session day I could. Higher doses bring certain challenges. Recall is a real challenge, and I developed a craft—what I call the craft—of remembering. When you go into new territory, you will lose some of those experiences. You won’t be able to bring them back in their entirety; there will be gaps in your notes. But going back to the same territory the next session and the next session, those gaps began to be filled in. I could bring back more of the experiences. You literally train your mind to stay cognitively coherent in new modalities of experience, new modalities of consciousness. There are two phases of every session, simply: there’s a cleansing phase and an ecstatic phase—the purifying phase, and then the teaching of the ecstatic phase. You’ll see this come up in my discussion.

I approached this entire task as a philosopher, with the rigor I had internalized—first as a historian and then as a philosopher. All the variables were standardized. The sitter for all of my sessions was the same sitter, with the exception of two sessions. Conditions were standardized, always starting at the same time of day, always isolated. I have never taken psychedelics and gone to a concert or gone for a walk in the woods. My work has been 73 high-dose sessions in a highly standardized environment. I kept methodical track of where I went, because even though individual sessions are hard to conceptualize, multiple sessions are even harder, since there is a trajectory of learning that takes place across them. I wasn’t trying to tell the story of my individual life. I was trying to look at the deeper cosmological or philosophical story. In high-dose sessions, the small circles represent personal life experiences. For me, personal experiences started at the beginning and came when I was coming back, but for the most part—after I got started—at the peak I was operating far beyond the limits of personal consciousness. It would touch into personal healings and dimensions, but for the most part I was operating outside personal consciousness once we got through the first five or ten sessions or so.

I kept rigorous records—track of the whole thing, all the dates, circumstances—and I even plotted the astrological variables. Once I began to understand astrology and it made sense to me, I began to use astrology to choose the dates of my sessions. I was looking for circumstances that gave me the greatest likelihood to be truly cathartic and also give me breakthrough potential. I also wanted to test Stan Grof and Rick Tarnas’s hypothesis that transits can be a predictor of the content of psychedelic sessions. One goes through many iterations of death and rebirth, which raises some pretty interesting questions—multiple levels of death and rebirth in a successive, increasingly intimate conversation or communion with the universe. The first death and rebirth is, of course, ego death. I’m particularly fond of Alex Grey’s representation of ego death; it jibes with my experience. It’s a total destruction of your time-space identity, of your physicalist identity.

When one goes into death after that, you are not simply repeating ego death. You’re experiencing deeper modalities of death that correspond to the deeper dimensions of reality. I’m using psychic, subtle, and causal here—there are other systems—but it’s not simply a repetition of ego death. It’s a different kind of death. This becomes such a theoretical issue that I’ve spent time thinking about: what is it that dies in those later deaths? If you’re interested, it’s in a paper on academia.edu—on life, death, and rebirth in LSD psychotherapy—asking, among other things, what dies in these advanced levels.

Let me give you the chapter headings and a few comments to give you a sense of where it went. Beginning with crossing the boundary of birth and death: if you know Stan Grof’s work, this is classic perinatal matrix—perinatal experiences. A lot of fetal sensation, a confrontation with the existential limits of life, intense physical purification—perinatal seizures, convulsions, detoxification of the body. Really rigorous—a hard physical workout—much more physically violent than I had anticipated, even knowing Stan’s work. I thought maybe it was because I was using such high doses; maybe not, I’m not sure. It culminated in ego death—having your life turned inside out and snapped like a twig—and it did it for me with exquisite ingenuity. I began the day as a white male, highly educated, obsessed with the meaning of life. Take each one of those and reverse them. I became female. I became people—women of color. I became philosophically trivial—no concern with that whatsoever—totally absorbed with the mundane conditions of life. And I became that amplified by 100, by 1,000—I became hundreds and thousands of women in circumstances that were not me. It was the universe’s way of saying: you think you’re this? Watch this. I’m going to turn you into the opposite. Now what are you? There’s nothing wrong with being Black, nothing wrong with being a woman, nothing wrong with being philosophically uninterested. What was wrong was the constriction that that identification—the set of social and physical roles—placed upon my consciousness. The message was: where you are going, those things do not apply. Let go and go beyond.

After a transitional period, I went into what I call the ocean of suffering—systematically, session by session over two years. That first round lasted about two and a half years. Over two years and 14 sessions, I was taken deeper and deeper into an ocean of collective pain, rage—unbelievable rage—suffering. It overwhelmed me. You have to learn and practice how to experience suffering of this magnitude—eventually taken into the very bowels of hell, worse than the literature suggests. I stopped my work in the middle for a six-year period. I did one year that took me into the ocean of suffering, stopped for six years, started again, and that session started exactly where the previous session had stopped—different time of life, different expectations, different astrology—yet absolute continuity. We picked up the work and it got worse and worse. You set your intention, but with high doses of LSD intentions don’t amount to much. I got to a point where I was begging for it to stop—enough was enough—but it didn’t. It kept getting worse.

This led me—behind my second book, Dark Night, Early Dawn—to try to answer the question: why this collective pain? Why so much suffering? Stan Grof’s theory is that collective suffering is drawn into a session because it resonates with ego death—drawn in by resonance—and when ego death finishes, the collective suffering disappears because there’s no death going on to anchor it. My experience was that I had already gone through ego death. This was different—a completely different modality of suffering. I eventually concluded it wasn’t aimed at my personal liberation at all. It wasn’t trying to heal me or detoxify my past. It was aimed at healing some dimension of the human collective unconscious. It was like the universe was using my sessions to detoxify the human species of some of the trauma of its history—its wars, murders, pain and suffering—coming through the sessions. As I’ve talked about this over the years, more psychedelic explorers have said, “Yes, that jibes with my experience too.” We think the universe is interested in our personal transformation—and it is—but the universe is equally, if not more, interested in the transformation of our species. If you will turn your work over—if you will turn your life over—to that, the universe will use it to accomplish its end. And right now, we are overwhelmed by the forces of historical transformation; they’re showing up in our sessions strongly. That’s the cleansing phase of those two years.

The next two chapters are the ecstatic phase of those same years. In the first year of the ocean of suffering, when I went into the ecstatic phase, I entered deep time and the soul. I experienced the whole of my life from beginning to end as a simultaneous, completed, finished product—all of it. It was like looking through a paper towel tube where all the details disappear and all time collapses; you experience the whole. I didn’t experience being old in some amorphous sense; I experienced the distilled essence of all the time-moments of my life. The first time I moved into this time modality, I came back and couldn’t remember. It was the most meaningful experience of my life, and it disappeared. It started coming to me in dreams; it dropped in while listening to music—my mind would open and I’d catch another piece. The next time I went into the state, I remembered more, and then more, and over four or five sessions I learned how to stabilize awareness in deep time—to stay awake, conscious, and cognitively coherent inside deep time. This is really important if you want to do any critical work with these states. You can train perception. You can train consciousness to operate in these conditions. And I want to mention, in that context—with deep gaso to you, Bill—I think ineffability is overrated as a marker of mystical experience. Yes, our experiences overwhelm our linguistic capacity, but if you keep going back and press the edge many times, your ability to articulate improves. You get clearer understanding of the complex weaving of experiences there.

I developed a technique. Always record the session within 24 hours. The next day you’re porous around the edges; your cognitive and verbal skills are back. I’d play the music of the session in exactly the order it had been played. With headphones, I’d replay the first hour over and over while writing, getting back on the edge of the state until I was sure I had captured all the experiences, then put on the next piece of music and do the same thing. It’s really working an edge—standing at the edge of the well—inside and outside, trying to record. Once I have the session recorded, I don’t change it. I don’t edit it. If I try to “improve” wording, I might distort something in the crudeness or structure of the original language. That’s why whenever I quote my sessions in any book, they’re italicized. They’re my Red Book—the core communion.

In the second year, after the six-year hiatus, I came back and went far beyond personal reality—no explanation given. I just entered a different level of conversation. I was taken on a series of initiations into the universe, a well-organized journey—every session built on the one before. I was taken deep into the beginnings of creation. I experienced creation as an act resulting from two divine beings—one that stayed outside time and space, the other that manifested as time and space and manifested the totality of Nirvanic chi, or what we are now. I had never anticipated that love—an extraordinary cosmic love—lay at the font of creation, but I was overwhelmed with love. One experience took me on a cosmic tour. There was an intelligence—never could see it, there were no images—but I was with this intelligence and it was showing me the universe. It would take me somewhere and show me something, and I would see it and understand; then it would take me somewhere else. I often wished I had a PhD in physics and astronomy—if I had those degrees, it could have shown me so much more. But it showed me what the philosopher could grasp—the philosopher’s dream of a lifetime.

Two sessions later I was taken on another tour, same kind of thing, but this time I stopped and asked, “Who am I with? Who are you?” I swooned, went through a kind of mini-death, and woke up in a deeper level of reality. There I discovered I was with myself—capital M. The process repeated. We were in a dialogue, and I asked, “Who am I with?” Every time I asked that—and this is relevant to entity encounters—I would die, wake up in a deeper reality, and discover I was in a deeper dimension of myself. This went on for two hours—dying and dying—until it became clear there was no outside to my being. This is nondual causal reality. All existence takes place within it. All perceptions of duality, all subject-object, are permutations within the vast expanse—what I call the Great Expanse.

In this period I began to be shown what I came to call the Master Story. In enormous expansions of time, far beyond the hundred years of my own lifetime, I began to be shown what appeared to be the intention for the human race—the large story that we were being driven from within to an evolutionary threshold of enormous significance. That theme built over the next five years. The initiation into the universe lasted about two years. Eventually, when the ocean of suffering came to a final culmination—hard to describe the magnitude of the absorption and transmission of collective suffering—it threw me out into what I call the Greater Real, archetypal reality. For the next year and a half I spent my sessions exploring the Greater Real. The form that death took to move into this level—and I can’t emphasize this enough—the ocean of suffering became the energetic foundation for everything that followed. The death and rebirth involved being immersed in liquid fire—white-hot lava flows—taken into the center of the sun and vaporized, shattered by enormous heat, liquid fire pouring through me, and yet somehow, while being destroyed at one level, continuity of awareness continued at another. I learned that continuity of awareness does not mean continuity of self or ego, but awareness continues.

Entering the Greater Real, my humanity began to fall away—a deeper form of death than ego death. In ego death you lose your individual identity; when your humanity falls away, you lose your human identity—a deeper level of identity. I moved into levels of reality beyond the collective consciousness—like a space station orbiting Earth, a completely different dimension of consciousness, hyper-clear. There I encountered what people would call the archetypes, but not Plato’s eternal ideas in the mind of an unchanging God. They were living, dynamic, in movement, vast—not nearly as small or anthropomorphic as Jung’s archetypes (and Jung is one of my heroes).

I encountered living beings so huge, so vast, the closest my mind could come to giving them form was as galaxies—millions of light-years across—beings responsible for generating time and space itself and some of the formative conditions within time and space. In subsequent sessions I was brought a little lower into what I call the low subtle level. I think of these living beings as high subtle reality. In the low subtle, I was taught all sorts of things about the collective tissue of humanity—both the collective body and the collective mind—in very precise detail, in different configurations. I began to have experiences of reincarnation not as an individual, but as an entire species reincarnating—every hundred years an entire species molting all the cells of its human beings and generating a new generation, and the intelligence of the articulateness of our collective incarnational process. I had always thought of reincarnation in individual terms, but here was the experience of an entire species and the intelligence that had brought this species into being pulsing—beginning to see that something was happening in history that was an expression of this intelligent collective pulse. Over the last several hundred years, and in the hundred years coming, there was a collective process that all our individual stories are connected to. I began to write about that in Dark Night, Early Dawn, in a chapter on collective karma—going beyond the story of the soul.

Eventually I wrote my third book, The Living Classroom. I never talked to my students about my psychedelic work—obviously, provosts do not like to hear that their professors of religious studies are doing massive doses of LSD on the weekends. I had a firewall: professor/shaman—don’t mix. But consciousness doesn’t respect those boundaries. I began to find that my students were being activated in the classroom in resonance with my psychedelic work. There was a porosity between their minds and mine. I would reach for an example to illustrate a principle and create an example that turned out to be from some student’s life, and it would touch them deeply. This began to happen regularly. Students had unusually deep experiences around concepts I was presenting—world religions, perennial philosophy. It seemed not the words, but that these experiences were now living inside me because of my work, and there was a contagion—fire lighting fire. I knew I was in trouble when I looked up and saw six students in a room of about thirty quietly crying. Enormous forces were involved. Eventually I came to understand it wasn’t simply resonance between their work and mine; there was a field in the room—created by the collective energy not just of the people present but of all the students who had ever taken the course with me. These fields were getting stronger over time, facilitating accelerated, deeper learning—year by year, decade by decade. I wrote The Living Classroom and didn’t mention psychedelics, because the point is the nature of consciousness itself. Anyone who works with any methodology that takes them deep into consciousness—where they touch the collective psyche—can expect to trigger these resonance experiences in those around them.

The following year I call the Benediction of Blessings. It was a year of extraordinary blessings. If the ocean of suffering was really hard to go through, the benediction was one phenomenal blessing after another. At this point I entered what I would call causal reality and lived there—many experiences of oneness and permutations of oneness, experiences of the fertile void, entering experiences where the entire physical universe, experienced from oneness, suddenly began to be erased, dropping into an absolute formless void underneath. There was a tremendous sense of closure. I felt completely satisfied. I felt like I had given something I had been asked to give in the ocean of suffering, and the universe had rewarded me more than I could have imagined. I felt complete closure. I had no idea what might happen next, but I wanted to find out, so I kept going. What happened next is actually the Diamond Luminosity.

One of the things that was wonderful listening to Berger today was his discussion of the hyper-dimensionality of time, because the deep time I went into in the early session that had to do with my life gave way to deeper modalities of time pertaining to the human species—not timelessness, not going outside time (that happened, but not here), but periods of like a hundred thousand years, a million years as a simultaneous present—not a billion, but a million years. I was being shown and taught what’s taking place over this long landscape of time. Eventually I gathered all the visions of the Master Story I had been given across those chapters, organized them, and told what was my visionary experience of what’s happening in history—where humanity is going, the crescendo we’re coming through in time. I was doing this work in ’94–’95. In one of these sessions—right in the middle of what I’m going into next—it took me into deep time, into the future, into the core of the death and rebirth of the human species, which I experienced not as a human being but as the species. Something about the work I had done allowed me to open up to some vast expanse of…

Which then brings us to some strong conclusions about LSD and DMT. Initiation into these realities is by becoming. You don’t take yourself into that reality and then have a transpersonal experience. The ego does not have a transpersonal experience. You become a citizen of that reality. You must let yourself die as what you are and dissolve into the wavelength, frequency, modality, or intelligence of that reality. How do we conceptualize the difference between the DMT encounter with so many beings and my experience with so few beings but so much being? We can talk about levels of reality—that’s part of it. We can also talk about morphic fields. Substances have morphic fields associated with them, I think. LSD is a relatively young psychedelic; it has a lighter morphic field, and therefore there is less collective programming associated with it than with psilocybin or ayahuasca. But what I’d like to focus on—perhaps the most seminal—is the participatory nature of psychedelic experience. Let’s step away from the idea that psychedelics transport you into a reality and then you experience that reality. I think there is a more subtle interplay between the mind which knows and the mind which is being known.

I’d particularly point to the role of death and dying in shaping the LSD interface, and to the significance of the long window. LSD is a pain in the ass because it keeps on going when you’d like to come back—you’ve seen enough for the day—but it keeps going. Short‑acting psychedelics are handy: quick access, quick out. But the long window—whether with DMT or 5‑MeO‑DMT in a snuff instead of smoking, or ayahuasca instead of snuff—allows a much more extended interplay between the consciousness you’re exploring and the consciousness that’s doing the exploring.

Here I draw upon the work of my friend Jorge Ferrer—his revisioning of transpersonal theory, articulating a post‑perennialist, participatory cosmology. We don’t simply discover the world that’s there. There is a participation; we elicit sets of experiences out of reality. We crystallize. It’s a participatory cosmology. My experience is that consciousness is an infinite ocean with infinite possibilities, many levels, and that when we take a psychedelic—or meditate, or do trance—the mind we drop into this ocean acts as a seed, a seed crystal that catalyzes out of the infinite potential a certain set of experiences. Those experiences change us. We drop our silliness, our pain; we are purged of the things that keep us small. We become purer—hopefully kinder, cleaner. When that seed crystal is changed and dropped back into that infinite consciousness, it catalyzes an even deeper communion, an even deeper set of experiences. We literally evoke the level of reality—and the citizens that live there—depending on the quality of consciousness we bring to it.

Maybe it’s like this: the less one is a being at the moment of contact with infinite potential, the fewer beings one will encounter. It’s almost like you have to be a being to encounter beings. If you’re a big being, you’ll encounter big beings. If you’re a small being, you may encounter small beings. What if you’re not a being at all at some point—what if you’ve let go so deeply? Conversely, the more you are a being at the moment of contact—that is, the fewer deaths you’ve undergone—the more beings you’re likely to encounter. I wonder if this might be part of what’s going on in the DMT interface: because it’s so fast, because it drops you out of your time‑space identity so quickly, it does shatter your egoic reality, and yet—as one presentation affirmed—your ego kind of comes with you. If your ego comes with you, that gives a certain structuring to how you experience reality at that level. The multiplicity of beings may be a fractal echo of the being‑quality we bring to it.

Ultimately this brings us back to levels. The world we enter with psychedelics is multi‑layered—multiple levels of intelligence. Beings exist relative to levels. When one moves into deeper and deeper states, just as your being falls away, beings fall away. When I moved into the diamond luminosity, there were no beings. There was just light. The amazing thing is that somehow my mind was there, but it was an infinite mind—just infinite, pure clarity. Between that infinite light and where you and I are right now, how many layers of reality are there, and how many types of beings populate those levels? I have no idea, but they’re vast in number. Thank you very much.
